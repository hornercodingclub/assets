I was looking at the snout of a , a particularly odd horned dinosaur, a rare relative of . It was not the first, or even the second, fossil of this creature found in Alaska, but I could already see parts of this skull that were not preserved on the other specimens. Continued excavation at the site--with picks and shovels supplementing our whisk brooms--yielded the bones and teeth of at least three other dinosaur species. It would take another trip for me to realize we were also crawling over 10 more skulls of . Most individuals were close in age and had probably died together in a flood or other catastrophe.
