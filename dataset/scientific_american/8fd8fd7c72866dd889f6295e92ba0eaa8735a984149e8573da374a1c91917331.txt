Read Our Latest Issue Before the smartphone or even Morse code, some rural peoples "spoke" long distance by whistling. Linguists are racing to study the dying languages One spring morning Panagiotis Kefalas was in the tavern he owned in the tiny Greek village of Antia when he received a call from his friend Kyria Koula. Kefalas was planning to have breakfast at her home some 200 meters from his tavern. The call did not begin with the sounding of a mobile ring tone. Instead it reached directly from Koula's mouth to Kefalas's ears, arriving in the form of a series of high-pitched whistles. This is a preview.
