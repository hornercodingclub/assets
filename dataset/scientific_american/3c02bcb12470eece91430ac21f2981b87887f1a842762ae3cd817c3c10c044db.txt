Read Our Latest Issue The adult brain generates neurons every day. These cells help us to distinguish one memory from another--a finding that could lead to novel treatments for anxiety disorders For centuries the notion that the adult brain could not make new neurons stood as a central tenet of neurobiology. Even Santiago Ramon y Cajal--the Barcelona-based histologist who essentially invented modern neuroscience at the end of the 19th century--declared such neural renewal impossible. After decades of careful observation and painstaking illustration of the microscopic architecture of nerve cells and their connections, Ramon y Cajal concluded that in the adult brain, "the nerve paths are something fixed, ended, and immutable; everything may die, nothing may be regenerated."
