Read Our Latest Issue Progress on the road to regenerating major body parts, salamander-style, could transform the treatment of amputations and major wounds A salamander's limbs are smaller and a bit slimier than those of most people, but otherwise they are not that different from their human counterparts. The salamander limb is encased in skin, and inside it is composed of a bony skeleton, muscles, ligaments, tendons, nerves and blood vessels. A loose arrangement of cells called fibroblasts holds all these internal tissues together and gives the limb its shape. This is a preview.
