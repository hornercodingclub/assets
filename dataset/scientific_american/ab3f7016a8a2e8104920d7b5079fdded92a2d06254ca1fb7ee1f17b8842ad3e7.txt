Read Our Latest Issue Thunderstorms give out powerful blasts of gamma rays and x-rays, shooting beams of particles--and even antimatter--into space. The atmosphere is a stranger place than we ever imagined Soon after the space shuttle launched a new observatory into orbit in 1991, Gerald Fishman of the NASA Marshall Space Flight Center realized that something very strange was going on. The Compton Gamma Ray Observatory (CGRO), designed to detect gamma rays from distant astrophysical objects such as neutron stars and supernova remnants, had also begun recording bright, millisecond-long bursts of gamma rays coming not from outer space but from Earth below.
