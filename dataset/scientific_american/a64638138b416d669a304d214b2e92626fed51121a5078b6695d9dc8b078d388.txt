The observations that led astronomers to deduce its existence could have another explanation: our galaxy lies at the center of a giant cosmic void In science, often the grandest revolutions are triggered by the smallest discrepancies. In the 16th century, based on what struck many of his contemporaries as the esoteric minutiae of celestial motions, Copernicus suggested that Earth was not, in fact, at the center of the universe. In our own era, another revolution began to unfold 15 years ago with the discovery of the accelerating universe. A tiny deviation in the brightness of exploding stars led astronomers to conclude that they had no idea what 70 percent of the cosmos consists of. All they could tell was that space is filled with a substance unlike any other--one that pushes along the expansion of the universe rather than holding it back.
